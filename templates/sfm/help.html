{% trans "What is WhoWasInCommand for?" %}

{% blocktrans %} WhoWasInCommand helps make the police, military and other security forces more transparent and accountable. There is a great deal of publicly available data on security forces but it is unstructured and scattered amongst a wide variety of sources, making it prohibitively costly for those engaged in public interest work to understand the security forces of a particular country. WhoWasInCommand reduces the impact of this problem by structuring public data on security forces and presenting it in a clear and auditable way. In doing this, we aim to empower and inform journalism, human rights reporting and other public interest work. {% endblocktrans %}

We’re a project of the Human Rights Institute at Columbia Law School and you can read more about us here. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Is WhoWasInCommand just about who was in command?" %}

{% trans "No. WhoWasInCommand can also tell you about:" %}

  • {% trans "The composition of security forces: how different units of security forces relate to each other in the organizational hierarchy or in the context of missions and operations." %}
  • {% trans "People’s non-command roles: we also document the ways persons can be connected to units in administrative and informal roles." %}
  • {% trans "Where units are based: we have data on facilities and installations like headquarters, camps and bases." %}
  • {% trans "Areas of operation: we collect data on where different units are reported to have operated, and make that available to you." %}

{% trans "Why have you singled out particular countries?" %}

{% trans "Good question. We take into account a number of factors when choosing which countries to research:" %}

  • {% trans "Are there longstanding concerns raised by civil society organizations and independent bodies regarding the human right records of security forces in a country?" %}
  • {% trans "Do journalists, lawyers and colleagues from human rights groups in that country agree that there is an information gap that is worth filling?" %}
  • {% trans "Is there a sufficient amount of publicly-available source material?" %}

{% trans "Presently, we publish research on Mexico, Nigeria and Egypt. We are actively researching additional countries informed by feedback from civil society." %}

{% trans "You’ve made a mistake. Who can I tell about it?" %}

{% comment %} Translators: Make sure to keep the HTML tags intact in this sentence. {% endcomment %} {% blocktrans %} Write to our research team at technical@securityforcemonitor.org and tell us about the mistake. We’ll investigate it further and make changes as required. You can help us speed up our investigation by sending us a link to publicly available sources that demonstrate the mistake. {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans %} The data in WhoWasInCommand mirrors information found in publicly available sources, which may not be correct and can contradict each other. This is why we grade the quality of every datapoint with a “confidence rating”. We assign ratings of “medium” and “high” to a datapoint when numerous sources from different publishers validate a datapoint. Until that happens, we our data given a rating of “low”. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "I have confidential information - can I leak it to you?" %}
